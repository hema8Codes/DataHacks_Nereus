## Version A — the 30-second elevator (memorize word-for-word)

Use this at sponsor booths and while waiting in line. Say it out loud until it's muscle memory.

> "Marine heatwaves are twenty times more frequent than forty years ago. They cost the aquaculture industry eight billion dollars a year. I built Nereus — a voice-driven 3D globe that lets a salmon farmer, an insurance underwriter, or a scientist ask questions in any of seven languages and get grounded, cited answers in under a second. Scripps data, curated fifteen-event catalog, Claude with a Gemini fallback. Ask it anything about the Pacific Blob."

That's it. Four sentences. **Practice saying it in 28 seconds.** If it takes 35, you're rushing your next answer.

## Version B — the 60-second hallway pitch (memorize the *beats*, improvise the words)

Don't memorize the 60-second word-for-word — you'll sound robotic. Memorize these **beats** and fill the words fresh each time:

| Beat | What you're doing | Approx. time |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Problem stat** | Drop the 20× frequency + $8B figure | 8 s |
| **2. Human story** | Name Chile 2016. "Twenty-seven million salmon." | 10 s |
| **3. Root cause** | "The data exists; nobody built the interface." | 8 s |
| **4. Solution frame** | "Voice-driven 3D globe, seven languages, real RAG." | 8 s |
| **5. Proof point** | *Click the globe. Let it fly.* | 10 s |
| **6. Market line** | "$300B aquaculture TAM. Three customer segments." | 10 s |
| **7. Ask** | "ML/AI + P&E. Happy to take questions." | 6 s |

**Drill:** Say it five times. Time each run. Your variance should be under 10 seconds by run 3.

## Version C — the 3-minute track pitch (beats with killer lines)

Same beat structure, inflated to 3 min. The killer lines below are the ones that must land **word-for-word**; the rest can breathe.

### Opening (30 s) — killer line:
> "The ocean doesn't send evacuation orders. I built one."

*(Two-second pause. Let it settle. Do not rush.)*

### Problem (30 s) — killer line:
> "A farmer in Puerto Montt, an underwriter in Zurich, and a scientist at Scripps all look at different data, in different languages, with no shared interface. Today."

### Solution + demo (90 s) — killer line (while the PDF generates):
> "Underwriters pay analysts three hundred dollars an hour to write this briefing. Claude wrote it in six seconds."

### Market + moat (20 s) — killer line:
> "The moat isn't the code. The code is forkable in a week. The moat is the curated Scripps-grounded corpus and the multilingual reach no one else in this niche ships."

### Ask (10 s) — killer line:
> "I'm here for ML/AI and Product & Entrepreneurship. Post-hackathon: three pilot cooperatives and one underwriter LOI by end of semester."

## Recovery moves — memorize these, they'll save you

### If the backend won't start
> "Let me show you the pre-baked briefing — the system has a deterministic fallback precisely because LLMs aren't reliable in live demos."
> *Open a cached PDF from `demo_outputs/pdfs/`. Point to the citation footer. Keep moving.*

### If the voice agent won't connect
> "I'll use text for this demo — the voice layer is ElevenLabs, and the judge next to you probably has the same connectivity constraint I do."
> *Type the next question. Don't apologize. Don't explain ElevenLabs. Move on.*

### If the globe won't fly
> "The globe's selection state is in the side panel — which is what an underwriter actually uses anyway. The globe is the hook; the dossier is the product."
> *Click the canopy directly. Scroll to the side-panel content. Pivot.*

### If you blank mid-sentence
> "Let me show you instead of explain."
> *Click anything. Let the product do two seconds of talking.* Then resume.

### If a judge interrupts
> "Great question — let me finish this thirty-second thread and then I'll give it the answer it deserves."
> *Finish your beat. Address the question. Thank them for it.*

### If you run over time
Don't apologize. Don't rush. Land the closing line slowly: **"The ocean doesn't send evacuation orders. I built one."** Sit with the silence. That lands better than a rushed "and also we have seven languages and—"

## The voice/body checklist (5 minutes before stage)

- **Breath.** Four in, six out. Do it three times. This lowers your pitch and slows your pace.
- **Water.** One sip. Not more — you don't want to need the bathroom during Q&A.
- **Posture.** Feet shoulder-width. Weight even. One hand at your side, the other on the trackpad only when you're clicking.
- **Face.** Half-smile. Not a grin. You're about to share something you're proud of, not beg for approval.
- **Eyes.** Pick three judges. Rotate. Two sentences per judge. Never talk to the globe.
- **Pace.** 140 words/minute. Faster = nervous. Slower = arrogant. Medium = confident.
- **Pauses.** After every killer line, count to two. *One Mississippi. Two Mississippi.* Then continue.

## Verbal tics to kill (out-loud rehearsal targets)

Record yourself saying the 60-second version. Listen. Cut these:
- "So…" as a sentence-opener → replace with the actual first word
- "Um" / "uh" → pause silently instead, 200 ms is invisible
- "Basically" → delete, it adds nothing
- "Actually" → delete unless you're correcting someone
- "I think" → replace with the direct claim; judges trust confidence
- Rising intonation on statements → practice landing declaratives firmly

---

## Killer transitions (use these verbatim to move between sections)

- Problem → Solution: *"So I built something."*
- Solution → Demo: *"Let me show you."*
- Demo → Market: *"Here's why that matters at scale."*
- Market → Ask: *"Which brings me to why I'm here."*
- Ask → Q&A: *"Happy to take questions."*

No "now I'd like to…" No "moving on…" No "as you can see…" Those are empty calories.

## Final rep before you walk on

Read out loud, once:

> "I know this product. I built every line. I can answer anything because I wrote it. If something breaks, I designed for that. The judges are humans who want me to succeed. I'm not begging — I'm sharing."

Walk on.
